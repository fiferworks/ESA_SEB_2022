Introduction

The Asian citrus psyllid, Diaphorina citri (Kuwayama) (Hemiptera: Liviidae) is one of the greatest threats to the citrus industry, because it acts as the primary vector for Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus (Alphaproteobacteria), the bacteria which causes Citrus Greening Disease or Huanglongbing (HLB) (Bové 2006). No cultivars of citrus have been found to be fully resistant to HLB (Graham et al. 2020). Infection eventually kills the host plant as bacteria damage the roots (Johnson et al. 2014b), causing branch necrosis, leaf yellowing, reduced tree yield, and poor development and quality of fruit (Bové 2006). Current citriculture methods are unable to completely control the psyllid and/or HLB, but the integration of best practices may slow the progression of the disease (Johnson et al. 2014a, Graham et al. 2020). The Dundee Biological Control Laboratory is primarily responsible for the rearing and release of Tamarixia radiata, a parasitoid wasp of D. citri nymphs (Qureshi et al. 2014). The production of our parasitoids used at FDACS-DPI is a synchronous rearing of wasps and psyllids, similar to those described by Skelley and Hoy (2004, Kerr et al. 2014, 2016). D. citri are raised on Orange Jasmine (Murraya paniculata (L.) Jack, Sapindales: Rutaceae) a true host of D. citri (Cheema and Kapur 1975), partially due to their relative resistance to HLB Walter et al. (2012b), their high rate of new flush after pruning (Hall and Albano 2014), as well as their relative attractiveness to D. citri (Ikeda and Ashihara 2008). As the D. citri develop, T. radiata are introduced to parasitize the nymphs, and adult T. radiata are collected post emergence for future field release (Kerr et al. 2016). Although M. paniculata is recognized as a good host for rearing D. citri, the Curry Tree (Bergera [Murraya] koenigii (L.) Sprengel, Sapindales: Rutaceae), is thought to be an original (and preferred (Chakrabarty et al. 1976, Singh and Nimbalkar 1977)) host plant of the Asian citrus psyllid (Qureshi and Stansly 2020). In addition, B. koenigii is thought to be fully resistant to HLB (Beloti et al. 2018, Athukorala 2020, Alves et al. 2021). Its volatiles are similar to those of M. paniculata (Yan et al. 2015), but are more attractive for D. citri (Beloti et al. 2017), to the point where this plant has also been considered for its potential as a trap crop (Marsh 2016).
